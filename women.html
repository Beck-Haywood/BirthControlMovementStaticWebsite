Leaders of The Birth Control Movement

Mary Dennet

“Mary Ware Dennett.” 3/4 Shot of Deceased Women's Rights Activist, Mary Ware Dennett, Ca.1892–1896., Courtesy of Schlesinger Library, www.radcliffe.harvard.edu/schlesinger-library /inside-the-collections/mary-ware-dennett.

Born: April 4, 1872

Died: July 25, 1947

"If a few federal officials want to use their power to penalize me for my work for the young people of this country, they must bear the shame of the jail sentence. It is the government which is disgraced, not I." (April 24, 1929)

—In response for being prosecuted by the comstock act for writing a pamplhet on sex education.

Wrote a famous pamphlet called “The Sex Side of Life” originally to educate her kids but was distributed later, leading to her being prosecuted by the Comstock act (1915)
Co founded the National Birth Control League (1921)
Rallied supporters to fight against laws that made birth control information illegal, like the Comstock Act (1930)
Summary: Taught the US accurate information on sex education, when there was no way to recieve it.

Katharine McCormick

"Portrait of Katharine Dexter McCormick (1908)"" Wanucha, Genevieve. “A Mind of Her Own.” MIT Technology Review, MIT Technology Review, 11 Feb. 2020, www.technologyreview.com/2011/02/22/196980/a-mind-of-her-own/.

Born: August 27, 1875

Died: December 28, 1967

"I have long wished to be of more constructive assistance to the Birth Control movement, especially along the lines of contraceptive research”(November 18, 1950)"

—In response to a letter from Margaret Sanger, where she asserted she will assist in the production of the pill.

Description: Katharine McCormick(left) fighting for womens sufferage Citation: “Rich, Famous, and Questionably Sane.” National Archives and Records Administration, National Archives and Records Administration, www.archives.gov/publications/prologue/2007/summer/mccormick.html.

One of the first women to graduate from Massachusetts Institute of Technology in 1904 with a degree in biology (1904)
Helped smuggle over 1000 diaphragms from europe into America (1920s)
Made large orders for diaphragms by posing as a german/french scientist (1920s)
Established the Neuroendocrine Research Foundation because her husband had schizophrenia and she believed it could have been cured with hormone treatment (1927)
Donated 2 million or (20 million today) to help develop the first oral birth control pill (1953)
Summary: Brought birth control into the US, and largely funded the birth control pill creating a important choice for women.
Katharine Dexter McCormick was one of the first women to study in MIT’s biology research lab. Citation:Wanucha, Genevieve. “A Mind of Her Own.” MIT Technology Review, MIT Technology Review, 11 Feb. 2020, www.technologyreview.com/2011/02/22/196980/a-mind-of-her-own/.

Margaret Sanger

"Portrait of Kathrine Dexter McCormick (1908)"" Wanucha, Genevieve. “A Mind of Her Own.” MIT Technology Review, MIT Technology Review, 11 Feb. 2020, www.technologyreview.com/2011/02/22/196980/a-mind-of-her-own/.

Born: September 14, 1879

Died: September 6, 1966

“No woman can call herself free if she does not own and control her body. No woman can call herself free until she can choose consciously whether she will or will not be a mother.”

—A powerful quote claiming women cannot be called free, until they have control for when they become a mother.

Coined the term birth control (1914)
Opened the first birth control clinic (1916)
Wrote books on birth control leading her to be prosecuted because of the Comstock Act (1916)
Founded the Planned Parenthood Federation of America (1916)
Summary: Changed the views of many on birth control by coining the term, opening a clinic to spread information, and fighting agaisnt the Comstock act.
Stewart, Nikita. “Planned Parenthood in N.Y. Disavows Margaret Sanger Over Eugenics.” The New York Times, The New York Times, 21 July 2020, www.nytimes.com/2020/07/21/nyregion/planned-parenthood-margaret-sanger-eugenics.html.
Controversy
Margaret’s belief on eugenics: Because of Sanger's position on eugenics, people believed she was racist. At the time many white eugenic promoters were claiming race should be a factor in preventing birth, however Sanger made it clear she did not agree with it.

“I admire the courage of a government that takes a stand on sterilization of the unfit and second, my admiration is subject to the interpretation of the word ‘unfit.’ If by ‘unfit’ is meant the physical or mental defects of a human being, that is an admirable gesture, but if ‘unfit’ refers to races or religions, then that is another matter, which I frankly deplore” (Katz, 1995, 47).

—Planned parenthood denounced her actions because it led to the Buck v. Bell case, causing 60,000 involuntary sterilizations.
